Designed at Capgemini Italia - Backelite.

The project was about the redesign of the user experience of an old, unintuitive internal management software. It was used to manage repair requests of complex company’s products. Before redesign and ux review, employees needed a long manual to understand how to use the platform and interact with it. Indeed, the challenge was to build an intuitive interface to help manage damages in an easier and faster way, without the need of a manual.

Overview

First steps were focused on research. I used the shadowing technique, observing a couple of users while interacting with the interface. We also did a kind of card sorting, together with a service designer, involving the client to prioritise the huge amount of information available in the giant “excel style” table of the homepage.

Then I did myself an expert review to better define where it was essential to simply and improve the interface.

Research

Once we had a deep understand of the context and the as-is interface, it was time for wireframe design phase.

I started with some low-fidelity wireframes design to collect employees’ feedbacks and when content and main structure were validated, I built a browsable wireframe to convey the interactions.

Design

The entire process ended with a usability test, to validate the entire work before its development and give meaningful feedback to the client.


I conducted the usability test with 15 users and I elaborated 3 different tasks per user role. The aim was to understand if employees were able to complete all tasks without frustration.

The test was very valuable. We found that users smoothly interacted with the interface. Even who was used to the old interface was able to understand and find what they were looking for.


This type of test provided also validation of design direction. It gave real behavioural insights on how users actually use the interface; it enabled everyone from stakeholders to see users’ use patterns and to find out their confusions or errors; last but not least, it provided insights and empathy for the user and it helped to fix design errors.

Here a couple of the main findings. Images represent heatmaps recorded during the test, where users gaze focused while completing the given task.

Test findings

In this first image is visible the new homepage design. The old version of this page, was designed with a huge horizontal and vertical scrollable table, with the possibility to be filtered per column, hiding not used ones.

I found that the new page, even if it introduced a different structure using cards and lateral filters, was really intuitive. Filters were a new introduced feature, but testers used them like they’ve always been there.

In this second image is visible the redesigned form. In the old version users left blank a consistent number of important fields because the huge amount of fields weren’t semantically related and they were also split in two columns.

In the new version I wanted to guide the user when filling out the form, that’s why I introduced the waterfall method. It was a success. It resulted simple to understand and follow. Fields were semantically grouped and not one of them was left blank.

Once collected the main findings, I drew up a report and shared it with the client. After a couple of months from the conclusion of the project, the client contacted my team to make us compliments and say how they’re work was improved thanks to our help.
