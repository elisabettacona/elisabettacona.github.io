The project was about the redesign of the user experience of an old, unintuitive internal management software. It was used to manage repair requests of complex company’s products. Before redesign and ux review, employees needed a long manual to understand how to use the platform and interact with it. Indeed, our main goal was to build an intuitive interface to help manage damages in an easier and faster way, without the need of a manual.

First steps were focused on research.

Shadowing. We observed some users while using the interface.

Card sorting. We involved the client to prioritise the huge amount of information available in the giant “excel style” table of the homepage.

Expert review & heuristic evaluation. We did some expert review and heuristic evaluation to better define where it was essential to improve the interface.


Then, it started the wireframe design phase.

Low fidelity wireframe. We started with some raw wireframes to collect employee feedback.

Browsable wireframe. Once content and main structure were validated, a browsable wireframe was built to convey the interactions.


The entire process ended with the testing phase.

Usability testing. To validate the entire work before its development and give meaningful feedback to the client.

Once we collected the main findings, we drew up a report and we shared it with the client. After a couple of months from the conclusion of the project, the client contacted us to make us compliments and say how they’re work was improved thanks to our help.

I conducted the usability test with 15 users and I elaborated 3 different tasks per user role. The aim was to understand if employees were able to complete all tasks without frustration.

The test was very valuable. We found that users smoothly interacted with the interface. Even who was used to the old interface was able to understand and find what they were looking for.


This type of test provided validation of design direction; it gave real behavioural insights on how users actually use the interface; it enabled everyone to see users’ use patterns and to find out their confusions or errors; last but not least, it provided insights and empathy for the user and it helped to fix design errors.

Designed at Capgemini Italia - Backelite.

Here a couple of the main findings. Images represent heatmaps recorded during the test, where users gaze focused while completing the given task.


In the first image is visible the new homepage design. The old version of this page, was designed with a huge horizontal and vertical scrollable table, with the possibility to be filtered per column, hiding not used ones.

We found that the new page, even if it introduced a different structure using cards and lateral filters, it was really intuitive. Filters were a new introduced feature, but testers used them like they’ve always been there.


In the second image is visible the redesigned form. In the old version the many available fields weren’t semantically related and they were split in two columns. We found that these were the main reasons why users left blank a consistent number of important fields.

In the new version we had to guide the user when filling out the form, that’s why we introduced the waterfall method. It was a success. It resulted simple to understand and follow. Fields were semantically grouped and not one of them was left blank.
